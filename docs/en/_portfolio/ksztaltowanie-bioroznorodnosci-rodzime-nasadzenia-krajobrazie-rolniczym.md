The project covers the following topics: design in open landscape, in agricultural landscape, counteracting the fragmentation of the natural environment through the creation of stepping-stone habitat networks, elimination of invasive alien species (IAS), native species, ecological succession, ecological corridors, supporting natural diversity, supporting animal species threatened with extinction.

The concept design, green space design and the selection of native flora species included therein were preceded by a natural report and were strictly based on the guidelines of the natural report entitled "Concept for the creation of a habitat for the red-backed shrike Lanius Collurio in the Głubczyce district".


## Project description

A planting project supporting a bird species – the red-backed shrike (Lanius collurio) in the agricultural landscape.

The project implemented in the area of the Głubczyce district is a response to the problem of strong habitat fragmentation in a landscape of distinctly agrarian character. Long-standing processes of agricultural land consolidation and crop monocultures have led to a significant impoverishment of biodiversity and the phenomenon of fauna isolation.

The main assumption of the study was to create sustainable and optimal living conditions for the red-backed shrike (Lanius collurio) – a bird species threatened with the loss of natural breeding sites as a result of the removal of mid-field shrubbery.


## Challenges

The main challenge was the strong habitat fragmentation in a landscape of distinctly agrarian character. Long-standing processes of agricultural land consolidation and crop monocultures have led to a significant impoverishment of biodiversity and the phenomenon of fauna isolation.

An additional problem was the presence of invasive alien species (IAS) in the area covered by the study. The tree inventory carried out revealed the presence, among others, of boxelder maple, green ash and walnut – plants planned for removal in order to protect valuable habitats from degradation and displacement of native flora.


## Solutions

Due to spatial conditions, including the impossibility of creating fully continuous ecological corridors, the core of the concept became the strategy of shaping stepping-stone habitats, the so-called "stepping stones". This system is based on the restoration and enrichment of traditional field margins and roadside greenery belts, which take the form of insular structures facilitating fauna migration between isolated patches of environment. This prevents the isolation of small populations, restoring the biological permeability of the agricultural landscape and protecting the soil from wind erosion.

The basis of effective habitat thinking in this concept is a rigorous selection of plant genera and species, strictly based on the guidelines of the natural report. In accordance with the recommendations of experts – biologists, plant species recognised as optimal for creating shelterbelts and safe breeding sites were introduced, including native wild rose, hawthorn and blackthorn, as well as fruit trees such as pear and plum. The thorny structure of these shrubs guarantees the shrikes physical safety and space to create characteristic "larders", while abundant flowering and fruiting provide invaluable benefit for insects and other birds, building a stable food base.

An important aspect of the study is the inclusion of natural ecological succession processes within the project framework. The practice of completely clearing the area of young self-seeded plants was rejected in favour of preserving inventoried, valuable, native self-seeded trees and shrubs, which as naturally selected organisms are characterised by the highest resistance to local conditions and climate change. Such action, supported by an extensive management plan limiting technical cuts to the necessary minimum and excluding intervention during the bird breeding season, allows for the creation of a rich ecotone – a self-regulating, resistant system in which landscape architecture deliberately and consciously gives way to natural environmental processes.


## Effects and results

Project objective: protection of the biological diversity of the Głubczyce district through the development of green infrastructure in urban and non-urban areas based on native species.

Project effects and results:
1) protection and development of habitats for local plant and animal species and preservation of biodiversity and protection of threatened species,
2) improvement of the living conditions of species inhabiting the habitat area of rare and endangered species,
3) increase in the tourist and recreational attractiveness of the district through the creation of fruit tree avenues and shelterbelts and the development of green areas,
4) improvement of the health and well-being of residents through access to green spaces,
5) increase in residents' environmental awareness, favouring a more sustainable lifestyle and better environmental protection,
6) improvement of ecosystem quality and preservation of biological diversity, contributing to the sustainable development of the region,
7) improvement of air quality, reduction of soil erosion and improvement of stormwater management,
8) prevention of climate change through carbon dioxide sequestration,
9) strengthening of social bonds and cooperation within the local community,
10) support for the local economy in the field of agritourism, local food production or gardening services.

The project "Protection of Biological Diversity of the Głubczyce District" is co-financed from the funds of the European Regional Development Fund under the European Funds for Opole Voivodeship 2021-2027 programme.
